Article collection "Cybernetic philosophy"


Level 1.1.4

The subjective stance and fatalism


We can perceive ourselves as agents who are free to choose any one out of several options to act, given all of those are within our reach. This belief itself makes a difference to our willingness to probe the range of options available to us in order to improve our situation. That we are "free" to choose can hardly be proved. Only one option will actually be executed, and if records of that action are taken, then one could argue that the action had been determined to be taken by the agent in that situation, given one finds other records of the past that suggest a causal connection between the events reported about in those records and the action taken by the agent. But the fact that one action has been committed is no proof that other actions had not been genuine alternative options for the agent at the time of the action. Actually, none of the two positions can be proved. So we are left with the belief that, while the past is fact-based, the future presents itself to us as open and manipulable, and this belief will make us actually try to engage in such manipulations to our advantage. As far as cybernetic philosophy is concerned, is makes no difference whether that belief itself has to have its origins in a free decision. It is sufficient that one holds it. That shows that the proactive approach of maintaining a self-model of oneself as a free agent does not necessarily contradict a belief in determinism. But belief is not objectifiable. It resides in my first-person perspective. In particular, my belief in future possible states of the universe implies a prospective stance that stands in stark contrast to the retrospective way of looking at facts. Facts can concern general truths, or they can relate to particular events of the past. The position that the future consists of facts as well is difficult to defend, since, as opposed to the past, there are no records of future events. One can only make an indirect argument: once the future has turned into present and then past, then there can be records of it, and therefore the events turn out to be facts. Again it seems there is a kind of stand-off in arguing in favour or against the reality of free will. But these metaphysical questions do not require a definite answer for the evaluation of the epistemic position of the cybernetic agent. For the agent and its task of evaluating actions that are supposed to influence future situations, a prospective stance commands itself. This prospective stance is nothing else but the aforementioned belief in an open and manipulable future, and this belief resides in the subjective perspective.
