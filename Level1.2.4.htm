Level 1.2.4

Physics of spontaneous processes


Why do things in general not run in our favour in the first place? Why do our purposes not realize themselves on their own? The ultimately underlying physical cause of this are probably the laws of thermodynamics, which govern how changes can happen in our physical world. While the first law states that the internal energy of an isolated system is constant, the second law dictates that the entropy of an isolated system increases in the course of any spontaneous change. Entropy is a measure of disorder of a system. Because of the second law, any system left to itself has the tendency of deteriorating from order to disorder. If the order is to be reestablished in the system, some intervention needs to be done from the outside into the system. In physical terms, "work" is performed in those cases. If system A performs work on system B, B's entropy can be reduced, which is not possible if B is left on its own. But if we consider the whole system comprised just of A and B, the second law imposes the constraint that the entropy of the composed system must rise during the process. Since B's entropy has been decreased, A's part of the entropy balance must have risen by at least the same magnitude to enable the process. Due to the first law, the energy of the combined system A + B stays the same, but the quality of its energy has declined in the course of the process. The share of the internal energy that can perform work, the so-called "free energy", has been decreased. Generally, doing purposeful things involves doing work, and the ability to do work requires having free energy at one's disposal. So getting hold of free energy is a necessary requirement to achieve one's goals whatever they are, and it is even the archetypical goal itself, as can be seen with some species of animals that forage for food, which contains free energy in the form of chemical energy, during a large share of their time.

The impingement of directed sunlight on the earth is an abundant source of free energy, which has enabled that life could evolve on our planet. But since most forms of agents cannot make immediate use of solar energy, the first law still entails the scarcity, or at least limitedness of practically usable energy in most situations, and the second law prohibits its reusal after it has once been used to do work and then dissipated into its environment. If one subscribes to the idea that what happens in the observable world is governed by the laws of physics, then the discussion so far helps to undertand the two constraints that we encounter in most situations: the scarcity of resources and the necessity of interventions when seeking to achieve one's goals.

The first three laws of thermodynamics can be written formally as such:

Zeroth law: If system A is in thermal equilibrium with system B, and system B is in thermal equilibrium with system C, then C is also in thermal equilibrium with system A. The property equal across all three systems is called temperature (denoted by T).

First law: The internal energy (denoted by U) of an isolated system stays constant.

Second law: Changes within an isolated system increase its entropy (denoted by S).

"Changes within an isolated system", if deemed phyically possible and not just hypothetical, are spontaneous, or entropic, processes. They imply in accordance with the second law that: \begin{equation} \Delta S_{total} > 0 \end{equation} Systems into which an agent intervenes or from which it draws its power are mostly open rather than isolated. So, while we are aware that the total entropy change that is implied by any physical process will be positive, it is more instructive to look at a physical quality that is more specific to the system itself, excluding the concomitant change in the surroundings: \begin{equation} \Delta F = \Delta U - T \Delta S \end{equation}

The quality F (free energy, or Helmholtz energy) immediately builds upon the previously introduced qualities. A system that can communicate with its surroundings via exchange of heat will try to minimize its free energy. If it absorbs heat from the surroundings, the increase in U will be outweighed by the concomitant increase in TS, with a net decrease of F during the process. On the other hand, a spontaneous process might occur in the system that either merely increases its entropy (and thereby reduces its free energy) without effects on the internal energy. Or a spontaneous process occurs that releases heat to its surroundings, which then also reduces the internal energy.

System changes describable by changes in F assume that the system's volume stays constant. A bit more relevant for biological examples and some examples from engineering are those that involve another quality, which is called Gibbs energy and whose change is defined by: \begin{equation} \Delta G = \Delta F - p \Delta V \end{equation}

By the corrective term \( p \Delta V \) we take into account the expansion work that the system might exercise or receive from its surroundings. Thus if a system expands during a spontaenous change, the above formula predicts that \( \Delta G \) will be negative.

In analogy to the second law, which describes spontaneity in an isolated system, the following two laws can be formulated:

At constant volume, a process is spontaneous if it corresponds to a descrease in Helmholtz energy.

At constant pressure, a process is spontaneous if it corresponds to a decrease in Gibbs energy

The relevance this has to agency is that spontaneous processes occurring in accordance with one of the two laws above not only sometimes release heat, but can also enable the extraction of work. As long as F (or G) is not zero, the system can in principle be used as a source of power for bringing about non-spontaneous processes elsewhere, i.e. for making purposeful interventions.

Free energy as a goal of actions
Disposing of free energy is necessary to be able to change the environment in a specific manner, and its consumption in the form of Gibbs energy is necessary for biological organisms in order to maintain their functionality. Obviously, organisms do not just "have" Gibbs energy. They can neither create it (due to the first law) nor reuse it indefinitely (due to the second law). So they have to acquire tokens of it. On earth we also do not simply have an abundant source of Gibbs energy that can be readily tapped by anyone that comes along. It is dispersed here and there, sometimes new tokens are created (by plants) by making use of the directed sunlight that impinges on the earth's surface. Scarcity of resources by default creates competition. In order to get hold of Gibbs energy, other competitors have to be outplayed. In order to outplay them, you have to have established for yourself a smarter policy of options (by learning), furnished yourself a better functioning organism (by growing one), or already brought yourself in an advantageous situation to begin with (by occupying a place on the playing field where you would not have ended up accidentally). All of these advantages for raising the chance of getting hold of Gibbs energy require having done purposeful interventions beforehand, which requires having consumed Gibbs energy. The Gibbs energy has been spent in order to create that purpose-machine that is other body and our mind, but in turn our body's and our mind's purpose is to acquire Gibbs energy. Whatever other characteristic we might want to supplement the concept of "goal" with, we already know (See Level 1) that it is a state that will not be reached by merely waiting until one drifts there. The interventions required to actually get there presuppose an apparatus that exerts them, and that apparatus will in turn not be in place by mere accident. Therefore, the goal of disposing of Gibbs energy (or other forms of free energy if we are not talking about organisms) can rightly be regarded as the primordial goal on which all other goals depend.
