Level 1.2.3

Cybernetics of higher-level agency


Being a higher-level cybernetic agent can be defined as being an agent that deals with multiple control circuits at the same time. Instantiations of higher-level agency are states, enterprises, and biological organisms, in contrast to parts of organisms that serve a single purpose, or mechanisms that control a single variable, like thermostats or Watt's steam pressure regulator. There are two senses in which the definition can be satisfied. In a horizontal sense, it means that the agent is of a certain complexity in virtue of being occupied with several control circuits. Different control circuits imply different target variables, which implies different goals. These goals can be independent states as such. But, strictly speaking, the goals with respect to an individual agent are never independent from each other since their attainment always implies for the agent the conflict of how to allocate the limited resources to them. But when understood in a vertical sense, there is a further dependence which is strictly one-directional, in the sense that there are lower-level control circuits whose functioning enables the agent to address problems from which the agent would otherwise be kept away.

A higher-level agent in the vertical sense is an agent that is ideally able to tackle tasks at different levels of detail by drilling down (see Level 1.3); other vertically higher-level agents are at least able to cause the completion of higher-level tasks by invoking services without being able to drill down into a finer-grained solution, should that be required. Higher-level agents, in general, are therefore agents that bring about solutions to superior kinds of problems, while they do not have to deal with the kind of problems that the lower-level agents are occupied with. Their focus lies not on optimizing the individual circuits, but on improving the configuration that exists between the different goals they pursue, and on reconciling the possibly conflicting strategies employed by the agent for the purpose of reaching those goals. While reconciliation in terms of a fair attribution of resources is the basic-level form of addressing the problem of improving one's goal-configuration, there are more proactive questions to be tackled in this context. What the agent should always seek is a configuration where the reaching of one goal provides a superior solution to a problem rather than just regulating a desired state of a system. These superior solutions are often identifiable by their feature of unlocking new kinds of problems that wouldn't even be addressable by the agent residing at the previous, inferior state.

There is the well-known distinction between "buy or make?", which spells the difference between being able to tackle certain problems at more fine-grained levels of detail, in case that necessity arises. In other words, the difference consists in: do I just invoke a service by spending money, or will I do the work (creating a desired function) myself? But between invoking a service and thereby spending money, and performing work there is not really a crisp line (the invocation of the service often includes works like searching for a supplier of the service, or the communicative efforts of specifying the service, checking whether the service has been provided in a satisfactory way, etc.); on the other hand when we ourselves perform a piece of work we often rely on lower-level services at some point, like bodily processes which are strictly speaking not "within our direct control"; also costs are always involved not only when invoking services, but also when performing work (via the expenditure of energy and time and therefore via not realising opportunity gains by spending these resources otherwise).

The horizontal aspects of the complexity introduced by higher level agency put the agent under pressure to address the need for setting up a goal system and reconciling the conflicts arising from the multiplicity of goals. The vertical aspects necessitate making decisions about which control circuits need to be put in place in order to allow the agent to continue to operate on high level. The higher level agent wants to keep a goal system that aligns the different goals in an optimal way and that avoids frictions among them.
